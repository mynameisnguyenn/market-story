## The Core Idea: Money Is Mostly Created by Banks, Not Printed

The instinct that money is "printed" by the government is wrong for the vast majority of it. **Broad money** (the total stock of spendable money) is created primarily by **commercial banks** — private banks that take deposits and make loans — when they lend. When a bank approves a mortgage, it does not hand over cash it took from savers; it simultaneously creates a new loan (an asset) and a new deposit in the borrower's account (a liability). That deposit *is* new money. The Bank of England's landmark 2014 paper made this official: roughly **97% of money** in a modern economy is bank deposits created this way; physical cash is about 3%. This is the **"endogenous money"** or "loans-first" view, and it overturns the textbook "money multiplier" story where banks merely lend out reserves.

## Measuring the Money Supply: M0, M1, M2

Economists slice the money stock by liquidity:

- **M0 / monetary base**: the most "outside" money — physical currency in circulation plus banks' reserve balances at the Fed. US currency in circulation was ~**$2.36 trillion** in April 2026.
- **M1**: currency plus checkable/demand deposits and other liquid deposits — money you can spend instantly. ~**$19.53 trillion** (April 2026).
- **M2**: M1 plus savings deposits, small time deposits (CDs under $100k), and retail money-market fund balances. ~**$22.80 trillion** (April 2026), growing 4.7% year-over-year.

The gap between the tiny base and the huge M2 is exactly the bank-created money described above.

## The Fed, Reserves, and the Price of Money

The **Federal Reserve** (the US central bank) does not set how much banks lend directly. It sets the *price* of short-term money and supplies **reserves** — the deposits banks hold at the Fed, the only "money" banks use to settle with each other. Post-2008, the US runs an **"ample reserves"** (floor) system: the Fed pays **interest on reserve balances (IORB)** — 3.65% as of April 30, 2026 — which sets a floor under interbank rates. The policy target, the **federal funds rate**, sits at **3.50%–3.75%** (effective April 30, 2026). The Fed's balance sheet stood at **~$6.7 trillion** (about 21% of GDP) on May 27, 2026, with reserves of **~$3.07 trillion** — down from a $8.9T pandemic peak after "quantitative tightening" ended December 1, 2025.

## The Intermediation Chain: Savers → Intermediaries → Borrowers → Real Economy

Money loops through the system (see the Sankey diagram below):

1. **Households and firms save**, depositing at banks and buying into **asset managers and funds** (pensions, mutual/ETF, insurers, money-market funds).
2. **Intermediaries** channel that capital. Banks lend directly; asset managers buy securities in the **stock market** (~$66–69 trillion US equity cap, early 2026) and **bond market** (US fixed income totals **$58.2 trillion**, the world's largest, including **$11.5 trillion** of corporate bonds as of Q4 2025).
3. **Borrowers** — corporations (for capex, payroll, buybacks) and the **government** — get funded.
4. Spending hits the **real economy** (wages, investment, consumption), and the income returns to households as savings, closing the loop.

## Treasury Debt Issuance

The **US Treasury** funds federal deficits by auctioning marketable debt. Debt **held by the public** was **~$31.27 trillion** in April 2026 (gross national debt, which adds intragovernmental holdings, was ~**$38.9 trillion**). Of the public total: **$15.93T notes** (2–10yr), **$6.62T bills** (≤1yr), **$5.37T bonds** (20–30yr), and ~$3.35T other. Primary dealers bid at auction; the debt then trades in the deepest securities market on earth and serves as the system's risk-free collateral.

## Repo: The Plumbing of Short-Term Funding

The **repo (repurchase agreement) market** is where the system funds itself overnight. A repo is a collateralized loan: one party sells a security (usually a Treasury) and agrees to buy it back the next day at a slightly higher price — the difference is the interest. This lets dealers, hedge funds, and money funds borrow cheaply against Treasuries and lend idle cash safely. The US repo market averages **~$12.6 trillion in daily exposures** (Q3 2025), ~69% collateralized by Treasuries. The benchmark rate, **SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate)**, was **3.66% on June 1, 2026**. Repo is the transmission belt that turns the Treasury market and reserves into system-wide liquidity — and when it seizes (September 2019, March 2020), the Fed intervenes fast, because a repo freeze starves everything else of cash.

## Why a Risk Analyst Cares

Every node here is a counterparty and a transmission channel. Reserve scarcity widens SOFR; a clogged repo market spikes funding costs and forces fire-sales; Treasury supply shifts the risk-free curve everything is priced off. Money is a flow, and your exposures live in that flow.
