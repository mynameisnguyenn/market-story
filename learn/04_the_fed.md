## What the Fed Is

The Federal Reserve System is the central bank of the United States, created by the Federal Reserve Act of 1913. It conducts monetary policy, supervises banks, and provides payment and liquidity infrastructure. For a risk analyst, the part that moves your book is monetary policy: the Fed sets the price of overnight money, and that price ripples through every discount rate, credit spread, and currency cross you hold.

## Structure

Three pieces, each with a distinct role:

- **Board of Governors** — 7 governors appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate to staggered **14-year terms** (the Chair serves a 4-year renewable term). The Board is a federal agency in Washington and sets the IORB rate and discount rate.
- **12 regional Reserve Banks** — Quasi-private district banks (New York, Chicago, San Francisco, etc.) that implement policy. The New York Fed runs the trading desk that executes open market operations.
- **FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee)** — The policy-setting body. **12 voting members**: the 7 governors, the NY Fed president (permanent vote), and 4 of the remaining 11 regional presidents on a rotating one-year basis. All 12 presidents attend and debate; only the rotation votes. The FOMC meets **8 times a year**.

## The Dual Mandate

Congress charges the Fed with two coequal goals: **maximum employment** and **stable prices** (plus an implied third, moderate long-term interest rates). "Stable prices" was operationalized in **January 2012** as a **2% annual inflation target**, measured by the PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) price index — not CPI. When the two goals conflict (e.g., high inflation and rising unemployment), the Fed must judge how far each is from target and over what horizon — the "balance of risks."

## The Tools

The Fed steers a **target range** for the **federal funds rate** (the rate banks charge each other for overnight reserves). As of the April 29, 2026 meeting, the range is **3.50%–3.75%**. It hits that target with administered rates rather than scarce-reserve scrambling:

- **IORB (Interest on Reserve Balances)** — Currently **3.65%**. The Fed pays this on bank reserves; no bank lends overnight below what it earns risk-free at the Fed, so IORB sets a near-ceiling/anchor for the funds rate.
- **ON RRP (Overnight Reverse Repo)** — Currently **3.50%**. The Fed borrows cash overnight from money market funds and others who can't earn IORB, putting a **floor** under short rates. Together IORB + ON RRP form the "floor system" that keeps the funds rate inside the range.
- **Discount window / primary credit rate** — **3.75%**, the rate banks pay to borrow directly from the Fed; a backstop ceiling.
- **Open market operations (OMO)** — The NY desk buys/sells Treasuries with primary dealers to manage reserve supply. The Fed cannot buy debt directly from Treasury.
- **QE / QT** — Large-scale asset purchases (easing) or balance-sheet runoff (tightening). **QT ended December 1, 2025**; since June 2022 the balance sheet shrank by ~$2.2T ($1.6T Treasuries, $0.6T MBS). The Fed is now reinvesting to hold reserves "ample."

## How an FOMC Decision Is Made

Staff circulate the economic outlook (the Tealbook). Over a two-day meeting, governors and presidents deliver assessments, then debate the policy rate and balance sheet. At four meetings a year (Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec) they publish the **SEP (Summary of Economic Projections)** and the **dot plot** — each official's anonymous rate forecast. The March 2026 median saw 3.4% by year-end with PCE inflation revised up to 2.7%. The Committee votes; **dissents are recorded**. The April 2026 hold drew an unusual **8–4 vote** — the most dissents since October 1992 — with one official (Gov. Miran) wanting a cut and three regional presidents opposing an easing bias.

## Transmission Mechanism

A funds-rate change propagates through four interlocking channels (see the flow diagram below):

1. **Interest-rate / credit channel** — The overnight rate resets SOFR, then bank prime, mortgage, auto, and corporate borrowing rates. Higher rates raise the cost of capital and tighten lending.
2. **Asset-price channel** — Rates are the discount factor for all cash flows; higher rates compress equity multiples and bond prices, lowering household wealth and spending (the wealth effect).
3. **Exchange-rate channel** — Higher US rates attract capital, strengthening the dollar, which cheapens imports (disinflationary) but hurts net exports.
4. **Expectations channel** — Forward guidance shapes expected future short rates, which is what actually prices the long end of the curve.

These feed **investment, housing, and consumption**, moving **aggregate demand**, and ultimately **employment and inflation** — with lags of roughly 6–18 months. That lag is precisely why the Fed acts on forecasts, not just realized data.
