## The US Financial Markets: Who's Who and How It Fits Together

The US equity market is the deepest in the world: total US stock market capitalization stood at roughly **$69 trillion at the start of 2026**, up nearly $7 trillion over 2025. Below are the structural participants you need to map cold. *(See the table and the order-flow diagram below.)*

## Exchanges

Exchanges are regulated venues where buyers and sellers meet; in the US they are themselves public companies (ICE owns NYSE; Nasdaq Inc. and Cboe Global Markets are listed). They run order books, set listing standards, and sell market data.
- **NYSE** (Intercontinental Exchange) — the legacy auction market, ~$31 trillion in listed-company market cap (2025).
- **Nasdaq** — electronic dealer market, home to mega-cap tech; its US listed-company market cap first surpassed the NYSE in June 2025 (~$32.0T vs ~$30.8T) and led for four consecutive months (June–September 2025), after which the NYSE was again the larger venue.
- **Cboe** — dominant in **options** (listed contracts giving the right to buy/sell) and operator of the VIX volatility index, plus four equity exchanges (BZX, BYX, EDGA, EDGX).

US trading is fragmented across ~16 exchanges plus dozens of off-exchange venues, so no single venue sees all flow.

## Market Makers / HFT and Broker-Dealers

A **broker-dealer** does two jobs: broker (executing orders as agent for clients) and dealer (trading from its own inventory as principal). FINRA regulated **3,184 broker-dealers** with **639,723 registered representatives** at end-2025.

**Market makers / high-frequency trading (HFT)** firms continuously quote two-sided prices, earning the bid-ask spread and providing liquidity. The retail wholesale niche is highly concentrated: **Citadel Securities handles ~35% of US retail equity trading and ~25% of all US equities volume**. Much retail flow reaches them via **payment for order flow (PFOF)** — wholesalers pay brokers (e.g., Robinhood) to route orders. Virtu Financial is the other major listed wholesaler.

## The Buy Side

The "buy side" deploys capital; the "sell side" (banks, dealers) intermediates and sells research/liquidity.
- **Asset managers (mutual funds + ETFs):** The US fund industry holds ~$45 trillion. **Mutual funds** (pooled, end-of-day priced) total $31.4T; **ETFs** (exchange-listed, intraday-traded) total $13.4T and drew a record $1.49T of inflows in 2025. The "Big Three" passive giants — **BlackRock ($14.0T), Vanguard (~$12T), State Street (~$5.7T)** — dominate index investing.
- **Pension funds:** ~$29.8T total; largest US public plan is CalPERS at $556B.
- **Insurers:** US life insurers hold ~$8T+ in general-account assets (the pool backing policy liabilities).
- **Hedge funds:** ~$5.2T globally; lightly constrained pools using leverage, shorting, and derivatives (AQR, Bridgewater, Citadel, Millennium).
- **Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs):** state-owned pools; Norway's GPFG (~$2.2T) is the world's largest single investor.
- **Retail:** individual investors, increasingly large via commission-free brokers and PFOF routing.

## Indices and How They Are Built

An index is a rules-based basket tracking a market segment.
- **S&P 500** — 500 large US firms, **float-adjusted market-cap weighted** (only investable shares count). Inclusion needs ~$22.7B+ market cap (since July 2025), positive trailing four-quarter earnings, and adequate liquidity; a **committee** selects members at its discretion.
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average** — 30 stocks, **price-weighted** (higher-priced stocks dominate), an outdated but iconic construction.
- **Nasdaq-100 / Composite** — Nasdaq-listed, market-cap weighted, tech-heavy.
- **Russell 1000/2000/3000** — purely rules-based by market cap; the Russell 2000 is the small-cap benchmark.

## GICS Sectors

The **Global Industry Classification Standard** (MSCI + S&P, 1999) sorts companies by principal revenue into a four-tier hierarchy: **11 sectors**, 25 industry groups, 74 industries, 163 sub-industries. The 11 sectors: Information Technology, Financials, Health Care, Consumer Discretionary, Communication Services, Industrials, Consumer Staples, Energy, Utilities, Real Estate, Materials. This is the standard language for sector exposure and factor attribution.

## Clearing and Settlement (DTCC)

After a trade executes, **clearing** (confirming/netting obligations) and **settlement** (final exchange of cash and securities, T+1 since May 2024) happen at the **DTCC**. Its subsidiaries — DTC (custody/settlement) and NSCC (equity clearing) — processed **$3.79 quadrillion** of securities transactions in 2024 and surpassed **$100 trillion in assets under custody** in 2025. DTCC is the systemic plumbing; a failure there is a market-wide event.

## Regulators

- **SEC** — primary securities regulator: enforces disclosure, oversees exchanges, funds, and advisers.
- **FINRA** — self-regulatory organization (industry-funded, SEC-overseen) policing broker-dealers and reps.
- **Federal Reserve** — monetary policy, bank supervision, financial-stability backstop; co-regulates systemically important clearing organizations.
- **CFTC** — regulates futures, swaps, and commodity derivatives.
