## From a Buttonwood Tree to Microseconds

The US equity market began as a cartel. On May 17, 1792, twenty-four brokers signed the **Buttonwood Agreement** outside 68 Wall Street, agreeing to trade only with each other and to charge a fixed commission of 0.25% of transaction value. This was a direct response to the Panic of 1792, America's first speculative bust, triggered by William Duer's failed cornering of bank stocks. The arrangement formalized into the New York Stock & Exchange Board in 1817. The lesson embedded at birth still matters to a risk analyst: liquidity is a club good, and access to it is priced.

## The Ticker Era and the Birth of Benchmarks

The 19th century ran on **open-outcry** (traders shouting bids and offers in a physical pit) and, after 1867, the stock ticker, which broadcast prices over telegraph wires and decoupled the market from the trading floor's physical walls. Quantification arrived in 1896 when Charles Dow published the **Dow Jones Industrial Average** (DJIA), originally 12 stocks averaged by simple arithmetic. In 1957 Standard & Poor's launched the market-cap-weighted **S&P 500** using punch cards, the first computer-generated index and still the benchmark most risk is measured against.

## Boom, Crash, and the Regulatory State

The Roaring Twenties bull market ran on **margin** (buying stock with borrowed money, sometimes 90% leverage). It ended in the **1929 Crash**: the Dow fell 12.8% on Black Monday (Oct 28) and another ~12% on Black Tuesday (Oct 29), and ultimately bottomed in 1932 down 89% from peak. The policy response built the architecture analysts still operate inside: the **Securities Act of 1933** (mandatory disclosure for new issues) and the **Securities Exchange Act of 1934**, which created the SEC to police secondary trading, insider conduct, and margin rules. Federal securities regulation is, fundamentally, a crash-response technology.

## Deregulation, Electronification, and Indexing

Three structural shifts in the 1970s reshaped market microstructure. **Nasdaq** launched February 8, 1971 as the first electronic quotation system, displaying competing market-maker quotes on screens (though trades still cleared by phone until 1984). On **May Day, May 1, 1975**, the SEC abolished fixed commissions, ending 183 years of the Buttonwood pricing cartel and unleashing discount brokerage and the cost compression that eventually produced zero-commission trading. And on August 31, 1976, John Bogle's Vanguard launched the first retail **index fund**, the intellectual descendant of efficient-market research at Chicago. Passive investing, near-zero at the time, is now roughly half of US fund assets, a regime change in who owns and moves the market.

## The Machines Take Over

**Black Monday, October 19, 1987** saw the DJIA fall 22.6% in a single session, the worst one-day percentage drop ever, amplified by **portfolio insurance** (automated selling of futures to hedge declines, which fed on itself). The response was **circuit breakers**, market-wide trading halts at 7%, 13%, and 20% S&P declines. The **dot-com bubble** peaked March 10, 2000 with the Nasdaq Composite at 5,048.62, then fell 78% by October 2002. Decimalization in 2001 (penny ticks instead of sixteenths) and **Regulation NMS** in 2005 (mandating routing to the best displayed price across venues) fragmented liquidity across dozens of venues and made **high-frequency trading** (HFT, algorithmic strategies competing on microsecond speed) dominant.

## Modern Fragility and the Retail Wave

The **2008 Global Financial Crisis** crystallized on September 15 when Lehman Brothers filed the largest bankruptcy in US history ($600B+ assets); the S&P 500 fell 38.5% that year. The **Flash Crash** of May 6, 2010 showed the new plumbing's fragility: the Dow shed nearly 1,000 points in minutes and recovered within ~36 minutes, after a single $4.1B futures sell program interacted with HFT liquidity withdrawal. The **COVID crash** of March 2020 was the fastest 30% drawdown on record (~22 trading days), tripping the Level 1 circuit breaker four times in two weeks before a March 23 bottom. Then in January 2021, retail traders on Reddit's r/wallstreetbets drove **GameStop** from ~$17 to $483, forcing Robinhood to halt buying amid a liquidity/collateral crisis. The arc runs from 24 brokers under a tree to millions of phones moving prices in microseconds, but the underlying dynamic—leverage plus crowding plus liquidity that vanishes when you need it—is unchanged.
