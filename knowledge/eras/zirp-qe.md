# ZIRP & QE recovery (2009–2015)

## What happened
After the GFC, the US entered an unprecedented stretch of **zero rates** and **quantitative
easing** to fight a slow recovery and disinflation. Asset prices recovered far faster than the
real economy — the start of the "QE bull market." Growth was tepid (~2%), inflation ran *below*
the Fed's 2% target for years, and the labor market healed slowly.
- **QE2 (2010–11)**, **Operation Twist (2011–12)**, **QE3 (2012–14, open-ended)** expanded the
  Fed's balance sheet from <$1T pre-crisis to **~$4.5T**.
- The **"taper tantrum" (May–Sep 2013)**: Bernanke merely *hinted* at slowing QE and the 10-yr
  yield jumped ~100bp, EM currencies sold off — a lesson in how addicted markets had become.
- Overlaps the **European sovereign-debt crisis** (see euro-debt) and the **2011 US debt-ceiling
  standoff** + S&P's downgrade of US AAA (Aug 2011).

## The Fed's decisions (Bernanke → Yellen)
- Held the funds rate at **0–0.25% from Dec 2008 until the Dec-2015 "liftoff"** — seven years.
- Three rounds of QE + Operation Twist to push down long rates and term premia.
- Introduced **forward guidance** ("low for an extended period," then date- and threshold-based)
  as a policy tool when rates were already at zero.

## The government's decisions
- **Dodd-Frank (2010)** — sweeping bank reform (capital, the Volcker Rule, the CFPB, living wills).
- Fiscal policy turned to **austerity** after 2010: the debt-ceiling fights, the 2013 **sequester**,
  and a brief **government shutdown (Oct 2013)** — a fiscal drag that left the Fed doing the work alone.
- The ACA (2010) reshaped healthcare.

## What it did to the US
- A long expansion and bull market, but **rising wealth inequality** (assets rose faster than wages) —
  a lasting political theme.
- Entrenched the idea of the **"Fed put"**: markets believed the Fed would always backstop them.
- Pension funds and savers were squeezed by zero yields ("financial repression").

## Read it in the data (timeline)
- **VIX** drifted structurally low (a calm, "buy-the-dip" regime), punctuated by the 2010 Flash
  Crash, the 2011 Euro/debt-ceiling spike, and the 2013 taper tantrum.
- **2s10s curve** stayed positively sloped and fairly steep — the textbook easy-policy shape.
- **10y yield (ust10)** ground lower (to ~1.4% in 2012) then jumped in the 2013 tantrum.

## Lessons / echoes today
- Low rates inflate *asset* prices long before *consumer* prices — the inequality engine.
- Markets get addicted to liquidity; *removing* it (taper) is more dangerous than adding it.
- "Lower for longer" can mean *years* — regimes persist far longer than they feel sustainable.
