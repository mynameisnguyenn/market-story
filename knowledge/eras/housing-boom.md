# Housing boom & easy money (2002–2007)

## What happened
The Fed's response to the dotcom bust — cutting to **1% and holding into 2004** — made credit
historically cheap. That, plus financial innovation (subprime lending, adjustable-rate "teaser"
mortgages, MBS/CDO securitization that *seemed* to spread risk), inflated a **nationwide housing
bubble**. Home prices roughly doubled 2000→2006; "house prices never fall nationally" became gospel.
Wall Street levered the whole structure. It all rested on the assumption home prices would keep rising.

## The Fed's decisions (Greenspan → Bernanke, 2006)
- Held **1% (2003–04)**, then hiked **17 straight times to 5.25% (2004–06)** — but slowly and
  predictably ("measured"), which kept long rates low (Greenspan's "conundrum") and didn't stop the boom.
- Largely dismissed bubble risk and the danger in subprime ("contained," 2007).

## The government's decisions
- A deregulatory, hands-off stance on derivatives and mortgage underwriting; encouragement of
  homeownership (Fannie/Freddie). Glass-Steagall's 1999 repeal let banks combine commercial and
  investment activities.

## What it did to the US
- A consumption- and housing-driven expansion built on leverage — and the direct fuse for the
  **Global Financial Crisis** (see gfc), which began as this era ended in mid-2007.

## Read it in the data (timeline)
- **VIX** sat at multi-decade *lows* in 2004–2006 (the "great moderation" calm) — the dangerous quiet
  before the storm; **2s10s inverted in 2006**, the recession warning the boom ignored.

## Lessons / echoes today
- The seeds of a crisis are planted in the *calm* — record-low vol and tight spreads are a setup, not a green light.
- Cheap money + financial engineering + a "this asset can't fall" belief is the recurring bubble recipe.
