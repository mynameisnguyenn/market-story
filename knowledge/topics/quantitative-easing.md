# Quantitative easing (QE) and tightening (QT)

When the policy rate is already at zero (**ZIRP**), the Fed can't cut further — so it eases by
**buying bonds** (Treasuries and MBS) with newly created reserves. This is **quantitative easing**.

## What it does
- **Pushes down long-term yields** directly (the Fed becomes a huge price-insensitive buyer) and
  compresses the **term premium**.
- **Pushes investors out the risk curve:** with safe yields crushed, money flows into stocks, credit,
  housing — lifting asset prices ("the portfolio-balance / reach-for-yield channel").
- **Signals** the Fed's commitment to easy policy (forward guidance reinforces it).

## The history (it's the spine of the modern era)
- **QE1 (2008–10):** invented in the GFC — ~$1.75T, the first balance-sheet expansion. See [gfc].
- **QE2 (2010–11), Operation Twist (2011–12), QE3 (2012–14, open-ended):** the ZIRP recovery; balance
  sheet from <$1T to ~$4.5T. The 2013 **"taper tantrum"** showed how addicted markets had become. See [zirp-qe].
- **COVID (2020):** *unlimited* QE + buying **corporate bonds** for the first time; balance sheet ~$4T → ~$9T. See [covid].
- **QT (2017–19, then 2022→):** the reverse — letting bonds roll off to shrink the balance sheet. QT
  has repeatedly "broken" funding markets (the Sep-2019 repo squeeze) — easing is far easier than tightening.

## The critique
QE inflates **asset** prices (owned mostly by the wealthy) long before **consumer** prices — a key
driver of the post-GFC rise in wealth inequality and the entrenchment of the **"Fed put."** The
2021–22 inflation is the bill for COVID-era money flooding a supply-constrained economy. See [inflation].

## Read it in the data
You can't see the Fed's balance sheet in our timeline, but its *footprint* is everywhere: the
structurally **low VIX** and **steep curve** of the QE years, and the **broken stock-bond hedge** when
QT/ rate-hikes made rates the driver (2022→). See: [zirp-qe], [inflation], [higher-for-longer].
